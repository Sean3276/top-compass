Data centres: the most demanding TOP profile in Singapore

A data centre goes through the same s.12 TOP/CSC process as any industrial building — and then through a second stack of regimes no other typology carries: capacity allocation, transmission-voltage grid connection, generation and electrical-installation licensing, bulk fuel storage, clean-agent fire suppression, cooling-tower registration and a dedicated Green Mark scheme. The base process is on the main dependency map; this page covers only what DCs add.

Before anything else: you need a capacity award

Since the 2019–2022 pause on new DC approvals, greenfield capacity is allocated by the state, not the market. The regime so far:

WhenWhatHeadline conditions
2019–2022Moratorium (implicit; ~60 DCs / ~357 MW at pause)—
Jul 2022 – Jul 2023Pilot DC-CFA: ~80 MW provisionally awarded (AirTrunk–ByteDance, Equinix, GDS, Microsoft) PUE ≤ 1.3 at 100% IT load; BCA-IMDA Green Mark (DC) Platinum; decarbonisation pathway
30 May 2024Green DC Roadmap: +300 MW near-term, +200 MW tied to green energy All DCs to reach PUE ≤ 1.3 within 10 years; new efficiency standards flagged
1 Dec 2025 – 31 Mar 2026DC-CFA2: ≥200 MW GM DC 2024 Platinum mandatory; PUE ≤ 1.25 at 100% IT load; ≥50% powered by eligible green pathways; SS 715:2025 (IT equipment efficiency)
1 Jul 2026Draft Digital Infrastructure Bill (consultation to 22 Jul 2026) Licensing regime for DCs and major cloud providers; PUE standards to apply to all DCs, existing and new; resilience/BCM/security duties put on a statutory footing

CFA conditions (PUE ceilings, GM DC Platinum) attach to the capacity award and its power/land arrangements — they are contractual award conditions rather than statutory TOP preconditions, and the exact evidencing deadlines sit in non-public award letters. The statutory hooks are the Green Mark minimums below and, once enacted, the Digital Infrastructure Act's licence conditions.

The DC overlay on the standard clearance matrix

RegimeWhat a DC addsWhen it bites
Grid connection (SP PowerGrid / EMA) Loads ≥ ~25.5 MW must connect at transmission voltage (66 kV+). Typical lead time from Consumer Connection Agreement to energisation: ~21 months (66 kV from an existing substation) to ~26 months (new consumer-provided substation, handed over ~8 months pre-energisation). Start of design — this, not any BCA step, sets the completion date. No permanent power → no IST, no fire T&C, no TOP inspection.
EMA electrical installation licence Required for non-domestic installations >45 kVA approved load; a Licensed Electrical Worker of the appropriate class takes charge. Completion/occupation stage — an operating licence in the TOP window.
EMA generation licensing Standby diesel gensets that are not grid-connected (island-mode, break-before-make) and <10 MW per the exemption order need no generation licence; grid-synchronised schemes (closed transition, demand response) lose the exemption (≥10 MW grid-connected → Generation Licence; 1–10 MW → Wholesaler licence). Design decision with licensing consequences at completion.
SCDF P&FM storage licence Bulk diesel (Class II petroleum) above exemption quantities needs a Petroleum & Flammable Materials Storage Licence — ~19 working days processing including site inspection, 110% bunding, validity up to 3 years. A multi-MW genset farm's fuel makes this a standing licence. Alongside the TFP/FSC chain; renewable in operation.
Fire Code 2023 — DC-specific relief For "communication nerve centres, data process centres and process control rooms", sprinklers may be replaced by clean-agent automatic extinguishing systems if the room has 1-hr fire-rated enclosure and doors, ≤2 exits, ≤15 m direct travel distance, and the system complies with Cl. 6.5.2. Every regulated product in that chain (agent hardware, dampers, doors, cables) needs a Certificate of Conformity — compile them at procurement, not at TFP time. Plan approval → T&C → RI inspection → TFP/FSC.
NEA — cooling & gensets Cooling towers are registrable aerosol-generating systems (mandatory since Mar 2022); monthly plate counts and quarterly Legionella testing, with results submitted to NEA since Oct 2024. Gensets: pollution-control study at BP stage, dispersion-modelled flue heights, off-road engine emission standards, factory boundary noise limits. Registration and test regimes start at commissioning.
PUB — water for cooling Users ≥60,000 m³/yr must submit an annual Water Efficiency Management Plan with private sub-metering. NEWater is the promoted cooling source; WUE is scored in GM DC 2024 (the Green DC Roadmap cites a 2.0 L/kWh benchmark for new DCs). Operational, but metering/design decisions are locked at design.
Green Mark Statutory minimum: any new building ≥2,000 m² GFA needs the BC(ES) Regulations minimum sustainability score, as-built compliance checked at completion. The DC-specific scheme — GM DC 2024 (Platinum ≈ PUE 1.25 at 100% IT load, measured after PDU; energy modelling at 25/50/75/100% loads; WUE scored) — is contractually required at Platinum for CFA winners, with final energy proof running on 12 months of operational data post-TOP. Design score at BP; as-built at completion; performance proof post-TOP.
IMDA / connectivity COPIF facilities (lead-in pipes, MDF/telecom rooms, risers) as for any development — joint inspection ≥6 months before TOP. DCs integrating cable landing stations add FBO licensing and the three designated landing sites (Tuas, Changi North, Tanah Merah). COPIF: 6 months pre-TOP. CLS: its own licensing track.
JTC (most DC land) Lessor consent for major works and fit-outs, plan endorsement, TOP/FSC documents returned to JTC within 14 working days, building-agreement milestones. Contract layer across the whole programme.

Completion sequencing: shell first, halls forever

The market-standard delivery model is powered shell / shell-and-core: the base building reaches TOP (often phased), and data halls follow as fit-out approvals with their own completion cycles — base-build compliance sits with the developer, fit-out compliance with the tenant, stitched together contractually so the integrated building stays compliant. Commissioning runs Level 1–5, with Level 5 Integrated Systems Testing proving redundancy under failure scenarios — IST is a market requirement that runs alongside, not inside, the statutory inspections, but it competes for the same commissioned-systems window and the same permanent power.

What actually delays DC completion here

  • The grid, overwhelmingly. 21–26 month energisation lead times collide with aggressive build programmes; industry commentary and the slow starts of the pilot-CFA winners both point at power infrastructure, not building approvals, as the pacing item.
  • Specialised equipment supply chains (switchgear, chillers, clean-agent systems) and the CoC paper trail for regulated fire products.
  • Late licensing starts — the EI licence, P&FM licence and cooling-tower registration are each fast individually but sit at the end of chains that are not.
