TOP vs CSC: what each certificate actually means

Both certificates come from the Commissioner of Building Control (BCA), and both relate to whether a building may be occupied. But they are not two names for the same thing — and the difference affects your payments, your defects rights, and your renovation plans.

TOP — Temporary Occupation PermitCSC — Certificate of Statutory Completion
Is it required? Optional. It exists so occupation can begin while minor items are still outstanding. Mandatory. Every project must end in a CSC.
What it certifies That outstanding non-compliance or incomplete work is "neither of a serious nature nor in any way detrimental to the well-being and safety" of occupants. It is only prima facie evidence of suitability for occupation. Full compliance with the Building Control Act and regulations, with every agency clearance and as-built plan approved.
What happens for buyers Keys and vacant possession; 25% of the purchase price falls due; the 12-month defects liability period starts at vacant possession. The final 15% of the purchase price falls due.
Deadline — For projects whose TOP was issued on or after 1 Jan 2023, the developer must obtain the remaining clearances within 2 years of TOP.

Why a building can be lived in before it is "fully approved"

Before a TOP is granted, the essentials are already cleared: fire safety (an SCDF Temporary Fire Permit or Fire Safety Certificate), water and sanitation (PUB), environmental health (NEA), planning-condition compliance (URA), lift permits, and BCA's own inspection. What gets deferred to CSC are items that don't affect safe occupation — typically road and street-works completion (LTA), landscaping (NParks), the final fire certificate, and survey plans. That's why living in a TOP-stage building is normal and lawful.

The legal backbone, in one paragraph

Section 12 of the Building Control Act 1989 prohibits occupying a building where building works have been carried out until a CSC is issued — except under a TOP. Occupying without either, or breaching a condition attached to a TOP, is an offence carrying a fine of up to S$50,000 and/or 12 months' imprisonment, plus up to S$2,000 per day if it continues after conviction. This is the developer's and owner's problem, not a buyer's — but it explains why nobody hands over keys a day before TOP.

What buyers should actually do with this knowledge

At TOP: collect keys promptly (your maintenance charges and, typically, final loan disbursements are tied to it), inspect thoroughly, and file defects in writing — the 12-month clock is running.

Between TOP and CSC: structural renovation is constrained — the building's as-built plans are still being finalised, and unauthorised alterations can hold up the project's CSC. Check with your MCST/developer and use contractors familiar with new developments.

If CSC drags: the final 15% stays in escrow-like limbo until CSC. A long TOP-to-CSC gap is common (12–24 months) and not by itself a red flag; a gap approaching the 2-year limit is worth asking your developer about.
