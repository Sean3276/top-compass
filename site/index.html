Three things buyers most often get wrong

1. TOP does not mean the building is fully approved. A TOP is an interim permit: the law (s.12, Building Control Act 1989) says it is only prima facie evidence the building is suitable for occupation. Full statutory sign-off is the CSC, which typically follows 12–24 months later — and since 2023 the developer must obtain it within 2 years of TOP.

2. Your money is staged around TOP and CSC. Under the standard Sale & Purchase Agreement, 25% of the purchase price falls due at TOP (that's when you get keys) and the final 15% at CSC. If completion is late, liquidated damages accrue daily at 10% p.a. on the instalments you have already paid.

3. The defects clock starts at vacant possession. You have a 12-month defects liability period from the date vacant possession is delivered. Document defects early and in writing — many buyers engage an independent defect inspector in the first weeks.
